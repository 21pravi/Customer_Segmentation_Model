# Methodology & Assumptions

Companion document to the Subscriber Segmentation & Churn Console. It records the
decisions taken during modelling, the reasoning behind them, and the limits of what
the results support.

---

## 1. Data provenance

The pipeline runs on a **synthetic subscriber base** generated by `src/data.py` from six
latent behavioural populations, with realistic data-quality defects introduced
deliberately: missing values, negative revenue arising from credit adjustments,
corporate and IoT usage outliers, and duplicate records from warehouse joins.

The feature schema, the business rules, and the modelling pipeline are
production-equivalent. Every metric reported is computed from an actual execution of
this code. What is simulated is the **input**, not the **results**.

Consequently:

- Methodology, model performance characteristics, and pipeline behaviour are meaningful
  and transferable.
- Absolute revenue figures (₹5.20 Cr monthly) and the 28.79% churn base rate describe
  the simulated population and must not be presented as observed commercial results.

Repointing the identical code at a production extract requires two changes in
`src/config.py`: set `USE_SYNTHETIC = False` and populate `COLUMN_MAP` with source
column names.

---

## 2. Population definition

Subscribers with **age-on-network below 90 days** are excluded (4,360 records, 2.2% of
the base). New subscribers have not established a stable usage baseline; retaining them
introduces a cluster that reflects tenure rather than behaviour, and contaminates the
centroids of the segments we care about.

The rule is enforced at inference as well as training. A subscriber presented for
scoring with AON below the threshold returns `UNSEGMENTED (AON < 90d)` rather than a
speculative assignment.

---

## 3. Preprocessing sequence

Order is load-bearing:

**Deduplicate → AON filter → invalid values → impute → cap outliers → engineer → scale**

- Deduplication precedes imputation so repeated records do not distort the medians used
  to fill missing values.
- The AON filter precedes imputation so values are imputed from the population that will
  actually be modelled.
- Imputation precedes capping because IQR fences require complete columns.
- Feature engineering follows capping so that ratio features are not constructed on
  uncapped extremes.
- Scaling is last.

### Missing values
Median imputation on `average_data_usage` (4,089 records) and `average_revenue` (2,718).
Median rather than mean: both distributions are right-skewed, and at this point in the
sequence the corporate-SIM tail has not yet been capped.

### Invalid values
Negative revenue (1,105 records) is floored at zero. These are credit adjustments — a
billing artefact, not subscriber behaviour. The subscriber is retained; the artefact is
neutralised. `average_dou` is clipped to the valid range [0, 30].

### Outlier treatment
Tukey fences at **k = 3.0**, not the conventional 1.5.

On a right-skewed telecom usage distribution, k=1.5 removes 2–3% of records, the
majority of which are genuine heavy-data subscribers and commercially the most valuable
population to identify. The k=3.0 fence isolates corporate and IoT SIMs specifically:
339 records on data usage, 417 on revenue.

Affected records are **winsorised, not dropped**. They represent revenue-generating
subscribers and belong in the segment counts, though not at their raw magnitude. The
fences learned at training time are persisted and reapplied at inference, so the
transform remains stationary.

## 4. Segmentation

### Algorithm selection

Two algorithms were fitted and compared:

| | K-Means | Gaussian Mixture |
|---|---|---|
| Silhouette ↑ | **0.4156** | 0.3922 |
| Davies–Bouldin ↓ | **0.9139** | 0.9304 |
| Calinski–Harabasz ↑ | **9,385** | 8,595 |
| BIC ↓ | not applicable | −629,263 |
| Soft assignment | no | **yes** |

They agree on 90.42% of subscribers under a best one-to-one matching.

K-Means scores marginally better on separation metrics. **GMM is deployed** because it
returns an assignment probability per subscriber. Mean assignment confidence is 0.9443,
and 5,594 subscribers fall below 0.60 — these sit near a boundary and can be routed to
manual review or excluded from high-cost interventions. A hard partition provides no
equivalent signal.

`covariance_type='diag'` is used: features are approximately decorrelated after scaling,
and full covariance estimation does not scale to the production row count.

### Choice of k

The kneedle elbow criterion and the silhouette maximum both indicate **k = 5**
(silhouette 0.4465).

**k = 6 is deployed.** This is a business constraint, not a statistical finding: the
campaign supports six distinct offers, and a sixth segment is actionable. The cost is
quantified rather than concealed — silhouette falls to 0.4156, a reduction of 0.031.
The decision should be revisited if the offer set changes.

### Segment naming

Mixture-model cluster identifiers are arbitrary. They permute across random seeds,
library versions, and data refreshes. Any fixed mapping from cluster id to segment name
will silently relabel the subscriber base the first time one of those changes.

Names are therefore attached to **mean-revenue rank**, a stable and ordered business
property. `cluster.profile_and_name()` computes the profile, ranks clusters by revenue,
and assigns names to ranks. The mapping is persisted alongside the model.

### Note on `pack_flag`

`pack_flag` is binary and is standardised along with the continuous features. This gives
it approximately one feature's worth of weight in the Euclidean distance, rather than
being dominated by revenue's raw scale. It is a strong splitting variable: two segments
(Sleeping Giant, Comfortably Numb) have 0% pack penetration and four have 100%. Segment
structure is partly organised around this axis, which is worth noting when interpreting
the profiles.

## 5. Churn model

### Why a second model exists

Segmentation is unsupervised. It has no accuracy, precision, or recall, because there is
no ground truth for a cluster assignment to be correct or incorrect about. Cluster
quality is assessed by separation metrics (§4).

Accuracy, precision, and recall require a label. The `churn` outcome provides one, and
the supervised model in `src/classify.py` is where those metrics apply.

### Selection

Three candidates were fitted and compared on ROC-AUC:

| Candidate | ROC-AUC | Accuracy @0.5 | F1 @0.5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logistic regression | 0.8193 | 0.7150 | 0.6071 |
| **Random forest** | **0.8239** | 0.7219 | 0.6119 |
| Hist gradient boosting | 0.8237 | 0.7850 | 0.5449 |

Random forest was selected. Note that histogram gradient boosting reports the highest
raw accuracy (0.7850) and the lowest F1 (0.5449) — a direct illustration of why accuracy
was not the selection criterion.

Features: the four segmentation variables, plus `aon`, four engineered ratios
(`revenue_per_gb`, `usage_intensity`, `revenue_per_active_day`, `is_dormant`), plus the
GMM segment as one-hot. Test split 20%, stratified.

### Performance

Held-out test set: 39,128 subscribers (11,266 churners, 27,862 retained). At the
F1-optimal threshold of 0.4978:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Accuracy | 0.7209 |
| Balanced accuracy | 0.7341 |
| Precision | 0.5102 |
| Recall | 0.7651 |
| Specificity | 0.7030 |
| F1 | 0.6122 |
| ROC-AUC | 0.8239 |
| PR-AUC | 0.6458 |
| Matthews correlation | 0.4279 |
| Cohen's κ | 0.4075 |

Confusion matrix: TN 19,587 · FP 8,275 · FN 2,646 · TP 8,620.

Five-fold cross-validation on a 60,000-row subsample: ROC-AUC **0.8179 ± 0.0045**,
F1 0.6053 ± 0.0072. The narrow fold spread indicates the test figure reflects stable
model behaviour rather than a favourable split.

### On accuracy

The base churn rate is 28.79%. A model predicting that no subscriber churns achieves
71.21% accuracy while identifying no churners and protecting no revenue. This is
reproducible on the dashboard by setting the threshold to 0.99, where accuracy reads
exactly 0.7121 and recall reads 0.

Precision, recall, PR-AUC and Matthews correlation remain informative under this class
balance and are reported in preference to accuracy.

### On threshold

The F1-optimal cut (0.4978) is reported as a default, not a recommendation. The correct
operating point depends on the ratio of retention-offer cost to the margin on a retained
subscriber, and should be set by the commercial team. The dashboard exposes the full
99-point threshold sweep with the confusion matrix, precision, recall, and the revenue
reachable at each point.

### Drivers

| Feature | Importance |
|---|---|
| `aon` | 0.243 |
| `average_revenue` | 0.230 |
| `average_dou` | 0.176 |
| `average_data_usage` | 0.073 |

Tenure and spend dominate, followed by engagement. This is consistent with standard
prepaid telecom retention findings and provides a sanity check on the model.

Importances are impurity-based and biased toward high-cardinality features. Permutation
importance on the held-out set is the appropriate follow-up before drawing causal
conclusions.

## 6. Segment surrogate

A random forest is trained to reproduce the GMM's partition from the four raw features:
accuracy 0.9940, macro-F1 0.9933, Cohen's κ 0.9924.

**This is a fidelity measure, not a correctness measure.** Its reference labels come from
the GMM. It quantifies how closely a fast tree ensemble reproduces the mixture model's
decision boundary, which allows new subscribers to be scored in milliseconds without
refitting the GMM. It says nothing about whether the segmentation itself is correct —
that question has no supervised answer.

It should be reported as a deployment-fidelity check. Cluster quality is reported in §4.

## 7. Campaign design

### Control group

A universal control group supports a causal read of campaign lift only if membership is
**independent of the outcome**. Selecting the holdout on any quantity correlated with
revenue or engagement — cluster confidence, spend, tenure — produces a control that is
not comparable to the treated population and biases every incrementality estimate
computed against it.

The 3% holdout is drawn **at random within each segment**. Stratification preserves
segment mix; randomisation within a stratum keeps assignment independent of behaviour.

Result: 189,771 UTG / 5,869 UCG.

### Balance verification

Standardised mean differences across all covariates. Conventional practice treats
|SMD| < 0.10 as balanced.

| Covariate | UTG mean | UCG mean | \|SMD\| |
|---|---|---|---|
| `average_data_usage` | 9.4594 | 9.4568 | 0.0004 |
| `average_revenue` | 265.97 | 266.05 | 0.0004 |
| `average_dou` | 19.099 | 19.091 | 0.0010 |
| `pack_flag` | 0.6722 | 0.6722 | 0.0000 |
| `aon` | 1,405.6 | 1,383.6 | 0.0216 |
| `churn` | 0.2881 | 0.2813 | 0.0151 |

All balanced. This check should be re-run on every refresh; an unverified control group
cannot support a lift claim.

The control group is never contacted. That is the purpose of holding it out.

## 8. Limitations

1. **Synthetic input.** Results demonstrate pipeline behaviour, not observed commercial
   outcomes. See §1.

2. **Churn is modelled as a static outcome.** No time-to-event structure, no censoring.
   A survival model (Cox proportional hazards, or a discrete-time hazard model) would be
   the appropriate next step if the business question is *when* rather than *whether*.

3. **No A/B validation of the offer book.** Offers are assigned by segment on the basis
   of behavioural profile and commercial judgement. The UCG exists precisely so that
   incrementality can be measured once the campaign runs; until it does, expected impact
   is not established.

4. **`revenue_at_risk` is an expectation, not a forecast.** It is Σ(revenue × P(churn))
   and assumes the predicted probabilities are calibrated. Reliability-curve calibration
   and, if required, isotonic or Platt scaling should precede any financial commitment
   based on this figure.

5. **Feature importances are impurity-based.** See §5.

6. **Cluster stability across refreshes has not been tested.** Bootstrap or
   Jaccard-based stability assessment (e.g. `clusterboot`) should be run before segment
   definitions are relied upon operationally.

## 9. Scaling

Executed here on 200,000 subscribers using a single CPU core. At the production scale of
~57 million:

- `silhouette_score` is **O(n²)** in time and memory. Already subsampled to 10,000
  (`SIL_SAMPLE`); scores are stable from ~10k upward (0.4153 → 0.4107 across 5k → 20k).
- `MiniBatchKMeans` for the k-sweep.
- GMM with `warm_start=True` over chunks.
- `HistGradientBoostingClassifier` rather than `GradientBoostingClassifier`. The
  histogram-based implementation bins features once instead of sorting at every split:
  measured 15× faster here (1.8s vs 28.7s). The exact implementation does not complete
  at production scale.
- `float32` / `int8` dtypes. Four `float64` feature columns at 57M rows occupy ~1.8 GB
  before any computation.
- Parquet in preference to CSV for intermediate checkpoints.
